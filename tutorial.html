Herbarium Tutorial

Follow our step-by-step guide to learn how to transform a fresh leaf into a preserved herbarium specimen.

Step 1: Collecting - What to Collect

Select Healthy Specimens: Choose plants that are flowering and pest-free. Include all relevant parts for identification: leaves, flowers, and seed pods.

Record Information: Note the collection date, location, and unique characteristics (smell, texture, height). Tracking bloom times over the years can be insightful.

Step 2: Preparation - Arranging the Specimens

Use Parchment Paper: Place the leaf between sheets of parchment paper, as it prevents color loss. For moisture-rich plants, use newsprint to wick away excess moisture.

Organize by Thickness: Keep plants with similar moisture content together. Arrange them to highlight key identification features, minimizing overlap.

Secure with Tape: Use strips of parchment and transparent tape to hold plants in place without tape touching the blooms.

Step 3: Pressing - Pressing the Specimens

Load the Press: Place the prepared plants in a plant press with parchment layers on both sides. Ensure even pressure while drying.

Monitor Moisture: After 24 hours, check for moisture. Replace wrinkled parchment if needed and adjust pressure.

Drying Time: Leave the plants in the press for 5-14 days, checking periodically. Remove dry specimens and readjust pressure for those that need more time.

Step 4: Mounting - Mounting the Specimens

Use Acid-Free Materials: Mount the dried specimens on acid-free paper using an appropriate adhesive (e.g., acrylic adhesive).

Minimize Contact: Use tweezers to handle the plants and apply adhesive to all edges to ensure a secure mount.

Final Placement: Press the mounted specimen to ensure contact and check for loose parts, applying extra adhesive as necessary.

Step 5: Freezing - Freezing the Specimens

Prepare for Freezing: Place mounted specimens between parchment paper and plywood dividers, wrapping them in plastic foil.

Freeze for 72 Hours: This step eliminates potential pests or fungi. The plastic protects against condensation while keeping specimens flat during drying.

Step 6: Identification - Identifying the Specimens

Record Observations: Document characteristics of the fresh specimen. Identify plants using a dichotomous key and compare with botanical illustrations for accuracy.

Utilize Resources: Use quality resources like “Flora Helvetica” for identification and a hand lens to examine fine details.

Step 7: Cataloging and Storage - Preserving the Specimens

Assign Numbers: Link each specimen to a digital database with photographs and information.

Protect and Label: Place specimens in acid-free sleeves with identification labels. Store in wooden cases with cedar shavings to deter pests.

Regular Inspections: Check for insect damage frequently, and refreeze if necessary for extermination.

Conclusion

Tips for Successful Herbarium Preparation

Always wear gloves while handling specimens to avoid contamination, and work in a clean area to minimize the introduction of pests.

In this tutorial, we explored the essential steps for preparing fresh leaves into herbarium specimens. We discussed the importance of proper techniques, storage, and maintenance to ensure the longevity and quality of your specimens. By following the guidelines outlined in this tutorial, you can create a valuable collection that contributes to botanical research and education.

We encourage you to practice these techniques and experiment with different plant species. Remember, the more you engage with the process, the more skilled you will become. If you have any questions or would like to share your experiences, please feel free to reach out.

Happy herbarium preparation!
